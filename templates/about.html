All about Coffee

The two coffee species that people primarily drink are Arabica and Robusta. Despite containing less caffeine than Robusta, Arabica beans are often considered superior in taste. Arabica tends to have a smoother, sweeter taste, with flavour notes of chocolate and sugar. They often also have hints of fruits or berries. Robusta, on the other hand, has a stronger, harsher and more bitter taste, with grainy or rubbery overtones.

Arabica coffee makes up 70% of global production while Robusta makes up 30%. Interestingly, our dataset consists of 98% Arabica-based coffees and only 2% Robusta-based coffees which may further indicate the preference of Arabica over Robusta. Arabica coffee tends to be sweeter and be close to the taste of chocolate, while Robusta is said to taste like burnt oil or burnt rubber.


Coffee Methods

Your morning cup of coffee all starts with a coffee cherry. From that cherry, several different possible methods can be used to get to the coffee beans you buy at the store. There are four different ways to process coffee, all of which change the sweetness, body, and acidity of brewed coffee. These methods are called natural process, washed process, semi-washed, and honey processed.

Natural Processing: The coffee cherries dry with the seed in the skin, then gets depulped. The results are juicy and syrupy coffee.

Washed / Wet Processing: The seeds get removed from the cherry, then washed of its mucilage, and then dried. This process gives a clean and crisp tasting coffee.

Semi-washed Processing: This method is common in Indonesia. Mucilage stays on the seed through the drying process. This results in a heavy-bodied, chocolatey, nutty coffee.

Honey Processing: Coffee cherries dry with the mucilage intact and leaves you with juicy, syrupy coffee with a more pronounced acidity.

Discover more on how coffee gets processed here: https://beannbeancoffee.com/blogs/beansider/coffee-processing-methods

Where Coffee Grows

Coffee is primarily grown in the equatorial zone - coffee plants prefer fertile soil, mild temperatures, rain and shaded sun. We found that the coffees included in our dataset followed this pattern. In the world map below, you can see how many coffees from each country are in the dataset. Any country that is gray in color and has a value of "undefined" does not have coffee in the dataset. One thing to note about this map is that most of the coffee produced in the United States comes out of Hawaii.
